<h2 id="case-1">I. Spatial Scale & Emotional Distance</h2>

### How Architecture Shapes Intimacy with the Past
Large memorial spaces often produce solemnity and respect. Yet respect does not necessarily create emotional closeness. Through repeated field visits, I began to notice that spatial scale, layout, and preservation directly influence how visitors relate to historical figures.
Space does not merely contain memory; it shapes the conditions under which emotion becomes possible.

### Case 1: The Mausoleum of Qin Shi Huang
At the Mausoleum of Qin Shi Huang, the long approach toward the burial mound is lined with pine and cypress trees. Stone slabs extend forward in alternating light and shadow, creating a restrained and dignified atmosphere.
In Chinese tradition, pine and cypress symbolize endurance and solemnity. The extended pathway slows movement, preventing immediate confrontation with the mound. Awe does not emerge from scale alone; it accumulates gradually through distance.
As I walked the path, my pace unconsciously adjusted. Silence, repetition, and open terrain made the emperor feel powerful yet unreachable. When sunlight shifted across the stone surface, moving shadows made time perceptible while preserving separation. Architecture mediates emotion step by step, producing reverence without intimacy.

### Case 2: Tombs of Generals and Statesmen
When shifting from imperial mausoleums to the graves of generals and statesmen, the emotional landscape changes. Some tombs are formally maintained by the state; others survive through local care or quiet neglect. Each spatial condition reflects a different relationship between historical reputation and collective memory.

#### Huo Qubing
Near the Maoling Mausoleum stands the tomb of Huo Qubing, marked by the stone sculpture Horse Treading on a Xiongnu Soldier. The site follows Han burial conventions and conveys national symbolism.
Standing before the sculpture, I sensed that the space guided visitors toward admiration and patriotic pride. The solemn arrangement leaves little ambiguity. Personal response is framed within official narrative. Emotional distance is maintained through symbolic clarity.

#### Bai Qi
The tomb of Bai Qi is simpler and more locally maintained. Ritual practices continue quietly, without grand architectural staging.
Without ceremonial framing, the encounter felt more direct. I stood near the tomb rather than approaching it through orchestrated procession. Emotional distance shortened. Reflection became personal rather than nationally scripted.

#### Li Si
Li Si’s tomb stands in an open wheat field in Henan, marked only by a modest mound. There are no pavilions or monumental structures.
In this expansive rural setting, imagination replaces instruction. The openness allowed contemplation of his political legacy and tragic fate without architectural direction. Simplicity created space for dialogue rather than prescription.

#### Lü Buwei
The tomb of Lü Buwei, located near an abandoned village, is marked by scattered bricks and overgrown grass. Few visitors arrive.
Here, emotional distance emerges not from grandeur but from neglect. The visible absence of maintenance emphasizes historical fading. Yet traces of offerings suggest memory persists quietly. The space reveals how vulnerability, too, can shape moral perception.
Across these tombs, scale and preservation produce distinct forms of intimacy or separation. State ritual, local continuity, rural openness, and abandonment each position the visitor differently in relation to the past.

### Case 3: From Monument to Participation
Not all memorial spaces rely on monumentality.

#### Feng Menglong Village (Suzhou)
Feng Menglong Village integrates traditional memorial architecture with interactive cultural programs. Visitors participate in storytelling, writing workshops, and performances inspired by his works.
Engagement here requires action rather than observation. Emotional connection emerges through participation. Instead of reverence from a distance, the space encourages embodied involvement.

#### Wang Yangming Memorial Hall (Shaoxing)
The Wang Yangming Memorial Hall presents the development of Yangming philosophy through curated exhibitions and intellectual lineages.
Unlike monumental tombs, this site does not rely on scale. Reflection arises through ideas. Moving through the exhibition, I felt intellectually engaged rather than overwhelmed. Emotional connection developed from understanding rather than architectural dominance.

### Concluding Reflection

Across these sites, I came to see that emotional intimacy with historical figures is neither automatic nor purely personal. It is shaped by spatial design — by how architecture positions the body, directs movement, and frames attention.
Monumentality can elevate the past beyond reach. Simplicity can reduce distance. Participation can transform memory into lived practice. Even neglect alters perception, making absence visible.

Through repeated visits, I realized that emotional distance is not determined solely by historical importance. It is constructed — physically and symbolically — through space.

<h2 id="case-2">II. Narrative Structure & Affective Direction</h2>

### How Exhibition Narratives Guide Emotional Response
If spatial scale shapes emotional distance, narrative structure shapes emotional direction.
 Historical sites do not simply present facts; they organize meaning. Through curatorial design, interpretive framing, and storytelling strategies, exhibitions guide visitors toward particular emotional responses.During my field visits, I began to notice that different narrative models produce distinct affective experiences. Some clarify meaning and direct admiration. Others suspend judgment. Still others allow emotion to grow gradually through local continuity or personal projection.

### 1. State-Centered Narrative

#### Heroic Focus, Clear Meaning, Directed Emotion
At the tomb of Zhang Qian in Hanzhong and the Gaoling Mausoleum of Cao Cao in Anyang, both developed under government leadership, narrative clarity is unmistakable.
Zhang Qian is officially commemorated as the pioneer who opened routes to the Western Regions. His perseverance, diplomatic endurance, and long-term political and economic impact are emphasized throughout the site. When I visited on a rainy afternoon shortly before closing time, the gardens, exhibition halls, and courtyards felt immersive and unified. The atmosphere did not invite ambiguity; it encouraged admiration. The narrative direction was clear: Zhang Qian represents national vision and resilience.
The Gaoling Mausoleum of Cao Cao presents a more complex figure. Historically controversial, Cao Cao occupies an ambiguous moral position in popular memory. Yet the newly constructed, solemn site reflects a measured and official stance. The broad layout, orderly exhibitions, and emphasis on archaeological discovery suggest institutional authority. I sensed an effort not to romanticize or condemn, but to stabilize historical interpretation. Even here, emotional direction is carefully calibrated.
In both cases, the state-centered model frames collective memory through structured meaning. Emotional response is guided rather than left open. Visitors are positioned within a shared national narrative.

### 2. Academic Narrative

#### Knowledge-Centered, Structured Context, Suspended Emotion
A different experience emerges at the Erlitou Site in Henan and the Stele Forest Museum in Xi’an.
These spaces prioritize archaeological data, historical chronology, and textual explanation. Exhibitions emphasize stratigraphy, artifact classification, and scholarly debate. The visual experience is comparatively restrained. Emotional impact does not arise immediately from atmosphere but from intellectual engagement.
At Erlitou, I found that my understanding depended heavily on explanatory panels and guided interpretation. Without context, the remains appear fragmentary. Similarly, at the Stele Forest Museum, rows of inscribed stone tablets require patient reading. The space does not guide visitors toward admiration or sorrow; it suspends emotional direction in favor of knowledge acquisition.
In these academic narrative environments, affect is deferred. Emotion emerges gradually through comprehension rather than through dramatic presentation. The visitor becomes a learner before becoming an admirer.

### 3. Family or Local Narrative

#### Personal Focus, Sustained Emotion, Meaning Through Continuity

The tomb of Zhou Yu in Lujiang, Anhui, presents yet another narrative structure.
Although the tomb is widely believed to be a symbolic burial site, it is carefully preserved and regularly maintained. Surrounding commercial streets and modern entertainment venues create a striking contrast. Just outside the walls, noise and activity dominate. Inside, the Huizhou-style courtyards remain quiet and orderly.
I have visited Zhou Yu’s tomb four times. Each time, I noticed careful restoration and a consistent atmosphere of respect. The site includes a small archive of family history and local documentation, where I once purchased a detailed regional gazetteer and a biography of Zhou Yu. Here, meaning is not imposed through monumental design but sustained through local remembrance.
The narrative is personal rather than national. Emotion grows through repetition, familiarity, and community care. Instead of directing a single interpretation, the space allows admiration to deepen through continuity.

### 4. Visitor-Centered Narrative

#### Participatory Focus, Open Meaning, Emotion Through Projection

At the Chibi Site Park and its historical museum, narrative shifts toward immersive participation.
The exhibition uses multimedia technologies—interactive screens, panoramic projections, and sound effects—to recreate the famous Battle of Red Cliffs. Visitors engage directly with digital simulations and reconstructed battle scenes. Standing near the actual landscape associated with the event, and then entering an artificially intensified audiovisual environment, I felt as though the historical fire was momentarily reanimated.
Here, emotion is not dictated solely by text panels or architectural solemnity. It is amplified through sensory immersion. Visitors project themselves into the narrative. Meaning remains open-ended but emotionally heightened.
This visitor-centered model reflects contemporary museology, where engagement becomes central to historical experience. Participation itself becomes a mode of remembering.

### Concluding Reflection

Across these four narrative models—state-centered, academic, local, and participatory—I came to see that emotional direction is never accidental. It is shaped by curatorial intention, institutional authority, and the degree of interpretive openness.
Some exhibitions clarify meaning and guide admiration. Others suspend emotion to foreground knowledge. Local memorials sustain feeling through continuity. Interactive spaces intensify emotion through projection.
If spatial scale constructs distance, narrative structure guides orientation. Together, they determine not only how we see the past, but how we are invited to feel about it.

<h2 id="case-3">III. Material Proximity & Tactile Imagination</h2>

### How Objects Mediate Touch and Historical Presence
If space constructs distance and narrative guides direction, materiality mediates touch — a more primary form of perception.
By “tactile imagination,” I refer to the process through which visitors mentally “touch” history when physical contact is restricted or mediated. Accessibility and inaccessibility, authenticity and replication, preservation and intervention together shape the intensity and form of this imagined contact.
Across museums and heritage sites, I began to notice three recurring modes of material encounter: protected originals behind glass, touchable replicas, and restoration workspaces where objects are actively being preserved.

### 1. Originals Behind Glass
#### Visible but Untouchable
In national museums, original artifacts are carefully isolated. Controlled temperature and humidity, protective glass, and regulated lighting ensure preservation. Objects are visually accessible yet physically unreachable. Distance enlarges their aura.
#### Hubei Provincial Museum · Sword of Goujian
The exhibition of the Sword of Goujian was strictly organized: single-file queuing, one-directional movement, limited stopping time.. Entry to the gallery required waiting in line for nearly an hour. Once inside, visitors were guided steadily forward. I stood before the sword for less than thirty seconds.
In that brief interval, I tried to observe the blade’s pattern, inscription, and condition. Staff members softly reminded visitors to keep moving. The encounter was fleeting.
Afterward, I searched extensively for information about the sword’s alloy composition, burial context, and preservation mystery. The inability to remain with the original object extended the encounter beyond the museum space.
The glass enclosure functions both as guardian and mediator. It preserves material authenticity while structuring the act of viewing. When touch is prohibited, imagination and research become emotional outlets.

### 2. Touchable Replicas
#### Experiential Contact, Suspended Authenticity
If glass enclosures amplify aura through separation, replicas redirect attention from authenticity to experience.
#### Nanjing Museum · Silver-Inlaid Bronze Ox Lamp
The silver-inlaid bronze ox lamp is a well-known artifact, but it is not always on display due to conservation concerns. After several visits without seeing the original, I encountered a full-scale replica in the museum’s cultural exhibition area, placed openly for visitors to touch.
I reached out. The metal surface felt cool; the engraved lines were slightly raised beneath my fingers. I knew it was a reproduction. Yet this knowledge did not weaken the experience. Instead, it allowed me to imagine how the original might feel. The replica did not deceive; it mediated.
Touch became a bridge between imagination and material sensation.
#### Luoyang Museum · Eastern Han Stone Bixie
At the Luoyang Museum, a sign near the original Eastern Han stone bixie directs visitors to a touchable replica near the exit. When I found it, children were touching its back while parents took photographs.
The replica closely mirrors the original in size and texture. I ran my hand across the carved surface. The rough stone and heavy contours corresponded with the visual memory of the artifact inside the gallery.
In that moment, I understood that replication is not necessarily a loss of authenticity but a redistribution of access. The replica allows forms of closeness that preservation protocols forbid. Emotional engagement shifts from reverence for originality to affirmation of participation.

### 3. Restoration Workshops
#### Intervention, Vulnerability, and Negotiated Continuity
Both isolation and replication assume that artifacts are completed objects. Restoration spaces reveal another dimension: material history as fragile and ongoing.
#### Beijing Leshi Cultural Relics Center · Bronze Restoration Internship
Over the past two years, I completed more than seventy hours of restoration internship at the Beijing Leshi Cultural Relics Center. When I first faced a Spring and Autumn period bronze ding, my instinct was to hold my breath.
The vessel was covered in green corrosion, with areas of active powdery rust. My task involved surface cleaning and localized rust removal. As I approached the object with a bamboo tool, my hands trembled — not from fear of failure, but from awareness that I was touching something handled over two thousand years ago.
The cleaning process was slow. I could feel the subtle resistance of corrosion beneath the bamboo tip and hear faint sounds as fragments detached. This slowness was not only technical but psychological. Reverence emerged naturally through contact.
Restoration revealed that artifacts are neither eternal nor stable. They survive through intervention. Each act of cleaning or reinforcement is a negotiation with time — among conservators, objects, and future viewers.
Today, many museums open restoration studios to the public or provide simulated experiences. Visitors observe or participate in simplified procedures. Such programs do more than explain technique; they reveal artifacts as vulnerable entities sustained through collective effort.

### Concluding Reflection
Across these three modes — protected originals, tactile replicas, and restoration encounters — material proximity proves complex.
Glass enclosures create visual access but prohibit touch, transforming distance into intellectual pursuit. Replicas permit contact while suspending authenticity, shifting emotion toward experiential participation. Restoration involves direct intervention, exposing vulnerability and shared responsibility.
Each mode carries trade-offs. Isolation strengthens aura but restricts sensory engagement. Replication enhances accessibility but complicates originality. Conservation extends survival yet alters material condition.
Material history is therefore not simply preserved; it is continuously negotiated. Our closeness to the past is never a straightforward arrival. It is shaped through mediation — between seeing and touching, authenticity and reproduction, permanence and change.
Perhaps this is the central insight materiality offers: historical intimacy is not a single act of contact, but an ongoing negotiation between present hands and inherited matter.


<h2 id="case-4">IV. Participatory Memory & Youth Agency</h2>

### How a New Generation Builds Connection with the Past
If space constructs distance, narrative guides direction, and material mediates touch, participation activates agency. Young people today are no longer only receivers of historical memory; they increasingly become active interpreters and reshapers of it.
Through social media, immersive experiences, and community practices, a new generation is building connections with the past in ways that are personal, embodied, and sometimes unexpected.
Across my field visits, I observed three recurring forms of participatory memory: digital self-inscription, immersive historical performance, and community-based ritual continuity.

### 1. Digital Self-Inscription
#### When History Meets Personal Expression
At well-known memorial sites, young visitors often record themselves through photography and short videos. These images circulate online, embedding personal presence into historical space. Documentation itself becomes a form of emotional inscription — a declaration of belonging within collective memory.
Beyond simple selfies, however, a more complex phenomenon is emerging: young people are actively rewriting their relationship with historical figures through humor, contemporary symbols, and personalized offerings.
#### Chengdu Wuhou Shrine · An Aircraft Carrier for Zhuge Liang
At the Wuhou Shrine in Chengdu, before the hall dedicated to Zhuge Liang, I noticed an unusual offering: a photograph of a newly designed aircraft carrier model. A young visitor explained to his friend, “Zhuge Liang spent his life fighting naval forces. If he had an aircraft carrier, history might have been different.”
This gesture contained multiple layers. It acknowledged Zhuge Liang’s military struggles while inserting contemporary technological achievement into the narrative. The offering was neither incense nor fruit, but a symbol of modern national strength — presented playfully yet sincerely.
The young visitor was not rejecting history; he was translating it. Humor became a medium of dialogue across centuries.
#### Anyang Cao Cao Mausoleum · “The Most Complete Ibuprofen Collection”
At Cao Cao’s mausoleum, social media users sometimes caption site photos with a joke: “This is where the most complete collection of ibuprofen in the country is located.” The reference alludes to Cao Cao’s famous chronic headaches.
Comment sections expand the humor: “Does Cao Cao receive deliveries?” “If he had ibuprofen, would Sima Yi still have won?”
This is not simple disrespect. By reframing Cao Cao’s pain in modern medical language, young visitors reduce temporal distance. The historical figure becomes human and relatable. Humor becomes a bridge — a quiet way of saying, “I understand your suffering.”
#### Shangcai Li Si Tomb · A Small Yellow Dog
At Li Si’s modest tomb, I once saw a small yellow dog toy placed among traditional offerings. The reference was clear: according to Records of the Grand Historian, Li Si’s final wish before execution was to walk with his yellow dog outside the east gate of Shangcai, chasing rabbits as he once had in youth.
Perhaps the visitor learned this story from a teacher, a short video, or a history book. Yet the gesture was unmistakable. The toy was not a joke; it was recognition — an acknowledgment of personal regret within political tragedy.
The smallest, most human detail became the strongest bridge across two thousand years.
#### Luoyang Bai Juyi Tomb · Returning Poetry to the Poet
At Bai Juyi’s tomb, I encountered handwritten copies of his poems left by young visitors. Some were carefully brushed in calligraphy; others were written on notebook paper and placed in envelopes.
Here, the direction of transmission seemed reversed. The poet once offered his words to the world; young readers returned them through rewriting. Copying became a form of inhabiting. Reverence transformed into intimacy.

### 2. Immersive Experience
#### Performing the Past
Immersive historical projects — role-playing events, reconstructed architecture, themed night tours — blur the boundary between spectator and participant. Visitors temporarily inhabit reconstructed historical environments. Emotional engagement becomes performative rather than observational.
#### Luoyang Yingtian Gate
At night, the reconstructed Yingtian Gate becomes illuminated. Young women in Tang-style dress ascend the steps while photographers adjust lighting behind them. The scene repeats across the plaza: dozens of visitors dressed in historical costumes moving through the restored structure.
There is no formal stage and no fixed audience. Everyone is both observer and performer. A university student told me, “When I wear Tang clothing and walk through this gate, I feel like I am inside history, not just looking at it.”
The experience may not produce academic knowledge, but it produces embodied imagination.
#### Xi’an Datang Everbright City
In Xi’an, Datang Everbright City transforms a modern commercial street into a Tang-themed environment. Lanterns hang overhead; performers dressed as imperial guards patrol the area. Yet the most striking image is the crowd itself — hundreds of young people in reconstructed historical dress.
A young woman adjusting her Tang-style hairpiece explained to me, “It’s fun. But you also feel the weight of the clothes. You understand something physically.”
Wearing historical clothing does not equal historical research. Yet it creates a bodily awareness that reading alone cannot provide. Fabric weight, movement restriction, and public gaze generate a sensory dimension of connection.

### 3. Community-Maintained Sites
#### Memory as Quiet Continuity
In locally maintained ancestral halls, small temples, and neighborhood shrines, participation takes another form. Here, memory is not spectacle but repetition.
#### Guan Yu Temples
Temples dedicated to Guan Yu exist across China, often maintained by local communities. In a small temple in Shanxi, I watched a young man light incense, bow, and leave. He carried a takeaway coffee in one hand.
“My father brought me here when I was little,” he said. “Now I come by myself.”
This was not performance for social media. It was habit. Memory continued through practice rather than explanation.
#### Local Zhuge Liang Shrines
In a small shrine dedicated to Zhuge Liang, I met an elderly woman sweeping the courtyard while her grandson arranged flowers.
“He is very wise,” the boy told me. He could not describe detailed military campaigns, but he knew this was someone his grandmother respected.
Here, historical transmission occurs not through textbooks but through family routines. Memory persists in quiet gestures.

### Concluding Reflection
Across digital self-expression, immersive performance, and community ritual, youth agency appears in multiple forms.
Social media transforms memorial space into a platform for reinterpretation. Aircraft carriers, ibuprofen jokes, toy dogs, handwritten poems — each represents negotiation between inherited narrative and personal meaning. Young visitors are not simply consuming history; they are translating it into their own language.
Immersive environments activate the body as a medium of historical engagement. Even if commercialized, they reveal a desire for experiential connection.
Community-maintained sites demonstrate that participation also survives in repetition. Lighting incense, sweeping courtyards, arranging flowers — these actions may be small, but they anchor memory in everyday life.
Critics may view humor as superficial, immersion as commercial, or ritual as outdated. Yet perhaps a more important question is this: when a generation reaches toward the past using its own symbols, humor, bodies, and routines, is that not memory continuing?
History does not survive only behind glass. It survives when each generation reactivates it — in its own language, in its own practices, and through its own forms of attention.
That is the core of youth agency: not the passive acceptance of a fixed past, but the ongoing effort to make the past one’s own.
