I travel across China not to see ruins, but to understand how the dead continue to shape the living.

Research Statement

This project examines how individuals actively seek emotional connection with historical figures within contemporary Chinese memorial spaces. Through fieldwork conducted across imperial tombs, scholar memorials, museums, and locally maintained heritage sites, I ask: why do I — and others — feel compelled to search for intimacy with the dead in certain spaces, while remaining emotionally distant in others? Rather than viewing emotion as passively shaped by architecture or official narratives, I approach memorial sites as interactive arenas where personal longing, spatial design, and narrative framing intersect. Visitors do not merely receive history; they enter these spaces with prior desires, moral imaginations, and cultural memories. Spatial arrangements and curated narratives may amplify, redirect, or fail to sustain this emotional agency. By analyzing the interplay between individual affective seeking and public commemorative structures, this project explores how historical memory becomes lived experience — shaping cultural belonging, ethical imagination, and contemporary identity. This framework is exploratory rather than definitive; it reflects an evolving attempt to understand how emotional intimacy with the past is structured in contemporary space.
