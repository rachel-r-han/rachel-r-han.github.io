I travel across China not to see ruins, but to understand how the dead continue to shape the living.

Tracing Cultural Landscapes

A field-based archive of spatial memory and heritage practice.

This project began with a question that followed me through many memorial sites across China.

Why do I sometimes feel unexpectedly close to people who lived centuries ago — and at other times feel almost nothing?

Through visits to imperial tombs, scholar shrines, museums, and locally maintained heritage spaces, I began to notice that emotional connection is not simply produced by architecture or official narrative. Visitors arrive carrying their own memories, moral expectations, and quiet longings.Some spaces seem to invite reflection and intimacy. Others feel distant, composed, or carefully staged.

The difference, I realized, does not lie in history itself, but in how space frames encounter. Rather than treating emotion as something passively shaped, I began to see these sites as places where personal desire and public storytelling meet.

I am interested in how historical memory becomes something lived — how spatial design can deepen, redirect, or quietly limit our sense of belonging in the present.

This framework remains exploratory, shaped by ongoing observation rather than fixed conclusions.
